How this is calculated

Everything below runs entirely in your browser on the data you loaded. There are four steps: turn your sales history into a demand curve, turn that curve into a pre-bake schedule, simulate many random days to see what the schedule actually does, then scale the result up to the totals shown above.

1 · Estimate demand

For each day of the week, every order in your data is placed into its minute of the business day (11:00–21:00 by default) and summed, then divided by how many of that weekday appear in your data. That gives an average toasts-per-minute curve for, say, every Saturday. A light 5-minute moving average smooths the curve so one noisy minute doesn't swing the schedule.

2 · Build the pre-bake schedule

The day is cut into consecutive blocks, each as long as your Freshness window. For each block:

  1. Add up the demand curve across the block to get its expected number of arrivals (call it μ, "mu").
  2. Using the Poisson distribution — the standard model for "how many random customers show up in a fixed span of time" — find the largest batch size q such that the chance of at least q customers actually arriving is still ≥ your Min fresh-sale chance to suggest. That's the last (most marginal) toast in the batch still clearing your bar for "likely to sell before it goes stale."
  3. Cap q at whatever oven capacity is actually spare: over one freshness window, your oven can physically produce oven_slots × (freshness_window ÷ average_bake_time) toasts; subtract this block's expected walk-in demand (μ) from that, and pre-baking may only use what's left over. Pre-baking never plans to take an oven slot away from a walk-in customer.
  4. The batch isn't put in the oven all at once — its q toasts are started one at a time, spread evenly across the block. Loading them all in together would tie up several ovens simultaneously right when a real customer might walk in; spreading them out keeps the oven's real, moment-to-moment spare capacity in line with what step 3 assumed.

3 · Simulate many random days (Monte Carlo)

For each day of the week, the tool runs Simulation runs independent simulated days, each stepped forward in 15-second increments. Every simulated day draws its own random number of arriving customers each step (Poisson-distributed around the demand curve from step 1) — and that exact same stream of customers is replayed twice: once for "doing nothing" (bake to order only, no schedule) and once "with pre-baking" (following the schedule from step 2). Using the same customers for both means any difference between the two is down to the strategy, not luck.

Within one simulated day, when a customer arrives:

  • If a pre-baked toast is already sitting ready (not yet stale), they get it immediately — the oldest one goes first, to minimize waste.
  • Otherwise, if an oven is free right now, they order fresh — but only if the total bake time plus hand-over time (Time to order/serve) is within their Wait before walkout; otherwise they leave empty-handed.
  • Otherwise every oven is busy. They'll end up with whichever happens first: a scheduled pre-bake finishing (no extra bake needed — they just get it once it's ready) or a walk-in-style oven slot freeing up (a brand-new bake starts for them once it does). If even the sooner of those two exceeds their patience, they leave without buying (a "walkout" / "balk").
  • Any pre-baked toast that sits unsold past its freshness window is thrown away (waste) — it cost its ingredients with no revenue to show for it.

4 · Turn simulated days into money, then scale to a total

Each simulated day tallies toasts sold, toasts wasted, and walkouts, then converts that to revenue (toasts sold × price) and profit (revenue minus the cost of goods for every toast baked, sold or wasted). Averaging across all simulated runs for a weekday gives that weekday's typical numbers for both scenarios; the gain from pre-baking is simply (profit with pre-baking) − (profit doing nothing).

Each weekday's typical numbers are then multiplied by how many times that weekday actually appears in your uploaded data (e.g. if you loaded 120 days, Saturdays show up about 17 times) and summed across all seven weekdays to produce the totals in the Bottom Line box above. Note: those totals scale to however much data you loaded, at that same mix of weekdays — if you upload less than a full year, "per year" really means "at this rate, extrapolated."

Terms used above

Freshness window
Minutes a baked toast stays sellable. Also sets the length of the planning block used to size each pre-bake batch (step 2).
Oven slots
Independent bake positions running in parallel.
Bake time — fastest / slowest
The range a single toast's bake time is randomly drawn from, every time one is baked in the simulation.
Min fresh-sale chance to suggest
The minimum acceptable chance that the very last toast in a batch sells before its freshness window closes. Higher = smaller, safer batches; lower = bigger batches with more risk of waste.
Wait before walkout
The longest total time — oven queue plus baking plus hand-over — a customer tolerates before leaving without buying.
Time to order/serve
Hand-over time added on top of baking, for every sale (fresh or pre-baked).
"Doing nothing" / baseline
The same simulated customers, but every order is baked fresh with no pre-bake schedule at all — the number pre-baking is measured against.
Walk-out sales recovered
(walkouts doing nothing) − (walkouts with pre-baking) — customers who would have left, but got a toast instead.
Waste
Pre-baked toasts never sold before their freshness window ran out.
Fresh-able windows (by-day table)
Informational only: how many toasts would be worth baking in a block if oven capacity were unlimited. Compare against Safe pre-bake/day, the real, capacity-respecting number the schedule and simulation actually use.
